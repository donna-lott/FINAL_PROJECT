Nuskhuri emerged later, around the 9th century, and was used in religious manuscripts, often in combination with Asomtavruli. Mkhedruli, the modern script, became dominant in the 11th century and is now the standard for writing Georgian. The Georgian language and its scripts hold a special place in the cultural heritage of Georgia. In 2015, the "living culture of three writing systems of the Georgian alphabet" was granted national status as intangible cultural heritage in Georgia. Placeholder Text 2: The Three Scripts of the Georgian Language The Georgian language is written using three unique scripts: Asomtavruli, Nuskhuri, and Mkhedruli. Each of these scripts has its own history, characteristics, and uses, reflecting the evolution of Georgian writing over the centuries. Asomtavruli is the oldest of the three scripts, with the earliest known inscriptions dating back to the 5th century. The name Asomtavruli translates to "capital letters," derived from the Georgian words aso (letter) and mtavari (principal or head). This script is also known as Mrgvlovani, meaning "rounded," due to its distinctive round letter shapes. Asomtavruli was primarily used for inscriptions and religious texts, particularly in the early Christian period of Georgia. Although it was gradually replaced by Nuskhuri in religious manuscripts, Asomtavruli continued to be used for titles and decorative purposes in later centuries. Nuskhuri, the second Georgian script, emerged around the 9th century as a graphic variant of Asomtavruli. The name Nuskhuri comes from the Georgian word nuskha, meaning "inventory" or "schedule." This script was primarily used in religious manuscripts, often in combination with Asomtavruli for illuminated capitals. The combination of Nuskhuri and Asomtavruli is known as Khutsuri, which translates to "clerical." Khutsuri was widely used in hagiography and other religious texts until the 19th century. Mkhedruli, the third and most recent script, became the dominant writing system for the Georgian language. The name Mkhedruli means "cavalry" or "military," derived from the word mkhedari, meaning "horseman" or "warrior." Mkhedruli first appeared in the 10th century and was initially used for royal charters, historical documents, and non-religious manuscripts. Over time, it became the standard script for writing Georgian, replacing Khutsuri in most secular contexts. Mkhedruli is a unicameral script, meaning it does not distinguish between uppercase and lowercase letters. However, a variant known as Mkhedruli Mtavruli, or simply Mtavruli, was developed for capital letters, though its use is now largely decorative. The three scripts share the same alphabetical order and letter names, but they differ in appearance and historical usage. Asomtavruli and Nuskhuri are primarily associated with the Georgian Orthodox Church, where they are still used in ceremonial religious texts and iconography. Mkhedruli, on the other hand, is the script used for modern Georgian and its related Kartvelian languages, such as Mingrelian, Laz, and Svan. The scripts are a testament to the rich linguistic and cultural heritage of Georgia, recognized both nationally and internationally for their historical and cultural significance.
